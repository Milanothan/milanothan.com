The Ancient Greek Computer: The Antikythera Mechanism

Jonathan Milano, August 14th, 2021

Somewhere around 87 BC, a Roman cargo ship hauling valuables to Italy met her fate off the shores of Antikythera just at the edge of the Aegean Sea. Among the many treasures onboard was one in particular that is considered to be worth even more than the Mona Lisa by modern scholars. The Antikythera Mechanism.

The shipwreck would remain undiscovered for centuries until the spring of 1900 when a crew of sponge divers on their way to the fishing grounds off North Africa elected to stop and wait for more favorable winds. Their layover was on the Greek island of Antikythera.

During their wait, the crew set out with copper helmet and canvas suit to explore the ocean floor and nearby depths. Descending 45 meters (148ft), diver Elias Stadiatis began to signal with haste that he was ready to be pulled to surface.

Once out of the water, he claimed to have witnessed heaps upon heaps of rotting corpses, both of men and horses. All of them, he said, strewn together amid the rocks of the seafloor.

Is this the skull of Mary Magdalene?

Jonathan Milano, May 1, 2021

Although Mary Magdalene’s historic existence is still under debate today, Christian text from sources including the New Testament tell us that Mary of Magdala (her home village on the shore of the Sea of Gailee) played a significant role in the life of Jesus Christ and the foundations of Christianity as it is known today.

According to legend, Mary was one of the women that stayed with Jesus even up to crucifixion. She is said to be the first one whom Jesus appeared to after resurrection. Other early Christian texts raise her status even more in that after the death of Christ she is considered almost an apostle rivaling the status of Saint Peter.

The New Testament tells us that the followers of Christ were prosecuted from the Holy Land after his death and many of them were forced out to sea in small ships without sail or rudder where they were left to perish on their own. The most common version of the legend states that Mary Magdalene was sent with Mary Salomé, Mary Jacobé, Martha, Lazarus, Maximin, and their Egyptian servant Sara into one of these ships.

The Ancient origins of the Tarot

Jonathan Milano, April 17th, 2021

The origin of the Tarot is a mysterious and scattered history that has arguably existed in one form or another since the dawn of civilization. Certainly that last sentence might raise the eyebrow of the modern historian; however new research is emerging that might soon change our knowledge on the origins of Tarot.

Today tarot is mostly known by the massive variety of decks available from crystal shops and online merchants. Most decks are now used for fortune telling and divination by practitioners of various faiths and beliefs.

The earliest known written records of the cards can be traced only as far back as 1367, and these records are 4 mostly regarding the banning of the cards. The first documented origin of the cards discovered was from Mamluk Sultanate, a medieval realm that covered Egypt, the Levant and Hejaz. This is usually the year that modern scholars agree the cards originated, not without debate.

That’s also when I inevitably glance at the comment section.

One unfortunately common cluster of comments I see on just about any post containing a human figure in an ancient Egypt group is that of the race of ancient Egyptians.
